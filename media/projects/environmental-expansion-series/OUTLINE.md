## Article 3: Methane and Refrigerants: Cutting the Non-CO₂ Climate Forcers

> **Type:** article
> **Date:** 2026-08-15
> **Proposed hero motif:** New generated variant: a hyper-ribbon splitting into two branches — one resolving into a methane molecule glyph, the other into a refrigerant fluid motif — both on warm paper with indigo light.
> **Target length:** 2,000–2,500 words

### One-paragraph summary

Methane and hydrofluorocarbon refrigerants are the most important non-CO₂ climate forcers after the energy sector. Methane's 20-year global warming potential is ~80–85× CO₂, and cutting anthropogenic methane 30% by 2030 could avoid ~0.3 °C of warming. The Kigali Amendment could avoid up to 0.5 °C by phasing down HFCs. But both problems are materials-limited: low-temperature methane-to-methanol catalysts do not exist at commercial selectivity, fluorine-free refrigerants must match thermophysical and safety constraints, and caloric solid-state refrigerants need low-hysteresis phase transitions. This article explains how corrected uMLIPs rank C–H activation barriers, scaling-relation-breaking catalyst sites, intermolecular potentials, and caloric phase-transition energetics.

### Key claims

- Anthropogenic methane emissions are ~360 Mt CH₄/year (~10 GtCO₂e at 20-year GWP); the Global Methane Pledge targets 30% reduction by 2030.
- Direct methane-to-methanol is a scaling-relation problem; corrected C–H activation barriers and selective field failure identify candidate breaking sites.
- Methane pyrolysis for turquoise hydrogen requires corrected carbon diffusion and nucleation barriers on molten-metal/alloy catalysts.
- HFC phase-down under Kigali could avoid up to 0.5 °C by 2100; fluorine-free replacements require accurate intermolecular potential and flammability screening.
- Solid-state refrigerants are limited by first-order phase-transition hysteresis; corrected transition-state and twin-boundary energies rank candidates.

> **Date:** 2026-08-22
> **Proposed hero motif:** New generated variant: a lattice fragment with alternating occupied and vacant sites, suggesting ion-selective recovery, overlaid with a faint fluorocarbon chain glyph dissolving into the indigo ribbon.
> **Target length:** 2,200–2,800 words

### One-paragraph summary

The energy transition cannot scale without lithium, cobalt, and nickel, yet primary mining is concentrated, slow, and environmentally destructive. Recycling, direct lithium extraction, and urban mining are materials-limited: selective sorbents, extractants, and direct-recycling reconstruction conditions are discovered largely by trial and error. In parallel, PFAS — "forever chemicals" — require selective sorbents and, ultimately, catalytic defluorination routes that do not yet exist commercially. This article links the two problems through a common computational need: accurate binding and activation energies in flexible, under-coordinated environments. Lupine's corrected ion-insertion energies, metal-ligand binding energies, and C–F activation barriers rank candidates for mineral recovery and PFAS destruction.

### Key claims

- Clean-energy mineral demand could grow 4–6× by 2040; the battery recycling market is projected at $35–50 billion by 2030.
- Selective Li⁺ recovery and Co/Ni separation are misranked by raw uMLIPs because guest-host binding energies fall outside bulk training distributions.
- Direct recycling of cathode materials requires corrected Li⁺ migration and oxygen-vacancy formation energies — the same correction used for LMR cathodes.
- PFAS remediation is a $5–10 billion market by 2030; the C–F bond (~485 kJ/mol) requires catalytic activation barriers that uMLIPs systematically underestimate.
- Corrected metal-fluoride thermodynamics predict catalyst poisoning and identify regenerable defluorination systems.

## Article 5: Cement, Concrete, and the Weight of the Built World

> **Type:** article
> **Date:** 2026-08-29
> **Proposed hero motif:** New generated variant: an amorphous, networked lattice fragment (suggesting cement hydrate) with the indigo hyper-ribbon threading through its pores, on warm paper.

### One-paragraph summary

Cement is responsible for ~8% of global CO₂ emissions, roughly 2.8 GtCO₂/year, and most of those emissions are process emissions from calcining limestone that cannot be eliminated by clean electricity alone. Alternative binders, alternative clinkers, and CO₂-cured concrete are promising, but their reactivity and durability depend on amorphous and metastable phases that standard computational methods struggle to predict. This article explains how corrected uMLIPs treat Si–O and Al–O networks, hydrate phase assemblages, and carbonate diffusion barriers — enabling a materials-first approach to cement decarbonization. It closes the series by arguing that Lupine's correction layer is a general platform for any material system where under-coordinated environments control performance.

### Key claims

- Cement emits ~2.8 GtCO₂/year; ~60% is process emissions from limestone calcination.
- Low-CO₂ binders (slag, fly ash, calcined clay, geopolymers) are amorphous or nanocrystalline; uMLIPs mispredict their reactivity without environment correction.
- Alternative clinkers require multi-component oxide screening across composition spaces too large for DFT.
- CO₂-cured concrete depends on carbonate formation and diffusion barriers at under-coordinated surface sites.
- The series' seven target areas share one conclusion: the materials bottleneck is a prediction-trust bottleneck, and a single correction-and-verification layer can address it across domains.
