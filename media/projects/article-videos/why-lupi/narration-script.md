# Why LUPI? — Narration Script

## Performance direction

Jessica; soothing, clear, and lightly technical, with the composure of a friendly robot explainer. Keep the pace brisk but never urgent. Treat LUPI as a tool shape, not a product pitch. Land the three jobs with quiet confidence. End on the open invitation. Avoid any mention of the parent organization or platform name beyond what the article requires.

## Beat sheet

### Beat 1 — The belief · 0:00–0:16

**Narration:** Molecular structures should not live behind a paywall, a download, or a command line. The moment someone wants to see what a prediction looks like, the tool should already be open in the browser. That is the idea behind LUPI: a lightweight, web-native molecular viewer that lets anyone rotate, measure, and share a structure.

**Visual cue:** A molecular model appears inside an open browser window, gently rotating; soft light suggests immediate, install-free access.

### Beat 2 — The problem · 0:16–0:31

**Narration:** Most molecular viewers are either too heavy to share or too simple to trust. Desktop tools are powerful, but they trap knowledge on one machine. Screenshot culture replaces real exploration. Lightweight web viewers often hide the coordinates, uncertainty, and context that make a structure meaningful.

**Visual cue:** Split scene: a heavy workstation chained to one building on the left, a pretty but oversimplified mobile viewer with fading coordinates on the right.

### Beat 3 — The middle path · 0:31–0:44

**Narration:** LUPI sits in the middle. Built for the web first, with enough fidelity to inspect real predictions and enough simplicity to drop into a paper, a slide, or a classroom. No installation. No account. No friction between the data and the observer.

**Visual cue:** A browser window at center holding a faithful molecular model; a paper, a slide, and a classroom chalkboard orbit softly around it.

### Beat 4 — Three jobs · 0:44–1:01

**Narration:** It has three jobs. Show structures as they are: load standard formats, render faithfully, and expose the underlying data. Make measurement effortless: distance, angle, and coordination environments a click away, not a plugin away. And let the claim travel: every structure can be linked, embedded, and inspected by anyone.

**Visual cue:** Three horizontal panels—exposed coordinate data, a measurement cursor clicking distance and angle, and a shareable link chain connecting embedded viewers.

### Beat 5 — The verification layer · 1:01–1:12

**Narration:** Under the hood, the viewer connects to a formalized makeability record: a growing chain of commitments, simulations, and certificates that say not just what a structure is, but why it can be made.

**Visual cue:** A pipeline from commitment documents through simulation certificates to a verified molecular model, with chain-of-trust links lighting up.

### Beat 6 — Why now, and the invitation · 1:12–1:40

**Narration:** Three things make a browser-native viewer the right shape right now. WebGL and WebGPU can render complex molecular scenes smoothly on ordinary devices. Generative models are producing more structures than ever, and each one needs a URL, not just a filename. Open science is pushing tools toward public, inspectable interfaces. LUPI is live. Try it at lupi dot live.

**Visual cue:** Three converging streams—GPU rendering, generative structures becoming links, and an open public interface—flow into the viewer.
