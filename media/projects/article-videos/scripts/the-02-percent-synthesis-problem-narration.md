# The 0.2% Synthesis Problem — Narration Script

Voice: soothing, clear technical robot (Edge TTS en-US-SteffanNeural). Calm, precise, warmly neutral; no slang, no sales energy.
Target pace: 145–160 WPM

## Beat 1 — Hook (0:00–0:10)

**VISUAL:** A field of 380,000 crystal tiles rushes toward camera, then collapses into just 736 glowing tiles. Giant counter resolves to **0.2%**.

**NARRATION:**
Okay, so AI predicted three hundred eighty thousand stable new materials. Huge, right? But by late 2023, only seven hundred thirty-six had actually been synthesized. That’s zero point two percent.

## Beat 2 — The real gap (0:10–0:25)

**VISUAL:** Split screen: pristine crystal model labeled **STABLE ON PAPER** versus a furnace producing a messy, fractured powder labeled **MADE IN PRACTICE**.

**NARRATION:**
And no, this isn’t just labs moving slowly. A crystal can look perfect on a computer and still have no practical chemical path into existence. Prediction is scaling. Synthesizability isn’t.

## Beat 3 — Four filters (0:25–0:49)

**VISUAL:** One candidate moves through four gates: **ROUTE**, **REAL FORCES**, **DISORDER**, **COST**. At each gate, failure modes peel away: competing phase, softened barrier, vacancies, wasted calendar and cash.

**NARRATION:**
Most screens skip four brutal filters. One: can real precursors reach the target instead of some competing phase? Two: are the forces accurate away from a tidy, equilibrium crystal? Three: what happens when the material has vacancies, disorder, or stacking faults—like real materials do? And four: what does failure cost? Usually weeks of lab time and thousands of dollars.

## Beat 4 — The error that flips the answer (0:49–1:02)

**VISUAL:** Energy-barrier curve shifts by **100 meV**; an ion’s hopping animation accelerates **50×**. Label flips from **INSULATOR** to **CONDUCTOR**.

**NARRATION:**
This is not a tiny accuracy problem. Miss an ion-migration barrier by one hundred milli-electronvolts, and the predicted hopping rate changes about fifty-fold at room temperature. You can literally call a conductor an insulator.

## Beat 5 — Correction, not generation (1:02–1:20)

**VISUAL:** A translucent correction field wraps around local atomic environments. A noisy energy landscape snaps into alignment; rejected candidates turn red before reaching a furnace.

**NARRATION:**
Lupine’s answer isn’t a bigger generator. It’s a correction and verification layer: an environment error field that measures where universal potentials go wrong, then corrects them at runtime with proper forces. Better candidates rise. Bad ones get flagged before the furnace turns on.

## Beat 6 — Climate stakes (1:20–1:37)

**VISUAL:** Battery pack expands into an EV and grid; a cobalt supply map highlights the Democratic Republic of Congo at **~70%**. Timeline races from 2035 to 2045 while cumulative emissions stack upward.

**NARRATION:**
That matters because batteries are tied directly to roughly twenty percent of the emissions cuts needed by 2030. Cobalt-free cathodes could also break a supply chokepoint: about seventy percent of cobalt comes from the Democratic Republic of Congo. And every decade of delay locks in emissions we cannot recover later.

## Beat 7 — Payoff / CTA (1:37–1:45)

**VISUAL:** Pipeline locks together: **PREDICT → CORRECT → SYNTHESIZE → CHARACTERIZE → SCALE**. End card: **From stable on paper to synthesizable in practice** plus article URL/QR.

**NARRATION:**
The goal is simple: predictions that flow to synthesis, characterization, and scale—not another generation cycle. Read the full article to see how we close the zero-point-two-percent gap.
