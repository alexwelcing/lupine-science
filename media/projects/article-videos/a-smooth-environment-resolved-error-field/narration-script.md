# A Smooth, Environment-Resolved Error Field — Narration Script

## Performance direction

Calm, precise, and measured. Treat the error field as a discovered object, not a sales pitch. Land the contrast between bulk and defect errors, then let the 0.906 result arrive quietly. End on the verification layer as the honest boundary.

## Beat sheet

### Beat 1 — Hook · 0:00–0:14

**Narration:** Universal machine-learned interatomic potentials promise near-quantum accuracy at a fraction of the cost. For bulk properties, they deliver. But on surfaces, vacancies, and planar faults—the structures that dominate real materials—they err by tens of percent.

**Visual cue:** A tidy fcc bulk lattice at full coordination resolves cleanly; it fractures into a surface, vacancy, and planar fault, each glowing with a red error halo.

### Beat 2 — The error landscape · 0:14–0:28

**Narration:** The failures are not random. Bulk observables stay within a few percent, while defect-family properties miss by fifteen to sixty times more. That asymmetry is the signature: the same error geometry appears again and again, tied to local atomic environment.

**Visual cue:** A split dashboard—bulk metrics in a tight green band, defect metrics in a wide red band labeled **15–60× WORSE**—with both bands converging on a single coordination axis.

### Beat 3 — The field · 0:28–0:44

**Narration:** The hypothesis is that the signed error is a smooth function of first-shell coordination, summed per atom. Bulk coordination fixes the error to zero. Three standard observables—two surface energies and a vacancy energy—anchor the curve. Then a fourth observable, the 110 surface energy, tests it blind.

**Visual cue:** A smooth indigo curve over a coordination axis, pinned to zero at bulk coordination and anchored by three amber knots; a shaded blind region extends toward coordination seven.

### Beat 4 — Blind test · 0:44–0:57

**Narration:** Across thirty-six model-material combinations, the field predicts the never-fitted 110 error with a correlation of zero point nine oh six. That result is not a fit; it is an extrapolation from measurements made elsewhere.

**Visual cue:** A predicted-versus-measured scatter plot resolves the fit line; a metric card locks in **r = 0.906 · n = 36**.

### Beat 5 — Run-time correction · 0:57–1:14

**Narration:** Invert the field, and it becomes an additive correction beside a live calculator. Analytic forces keep simulations consistent. In tests, nickel 110 surface error drops from nine point seven to one point five percent, while bulk lattice constants remain untouched and molecular dynamics runs with only fifteen point six percent overhead.

**Visual cue:** A compact pipeline flows from calculator through additive correction to analytic forces; before-and-after bars shrink **Ni: 9.7% → 1.5%** as the bulk lattice stays registered.

### Beat 6 — Provable boundaries · 1:14–1:27

**Narration:** The correction does not claim universal power. Where rankings invert, a machine-checked Lean 4 proof shows no monotone correction can recover both. The proof kernel locks the data, the arithmetic, and the stated inequalities.

**Visual cue:** A proof ribbon stamps claims as **SUPPORTED**, **BOUNDED**, or **OUT OF DOMAIN**; a ranking-inversion pair is gently moved into the red out-of-domain zone.

### Beat 7 — Thesis · 1:27–1:40

**Narration:** The result is a concrete instance of a larger pattern: measure the structured wrongness of a predictor, correct it at run time, and prove exactly where the method does and does not apply.

**Visual cue:** The measure-correct-prove cycle closes around the coordination-error field, then resolves to a clean closing thesis card.
